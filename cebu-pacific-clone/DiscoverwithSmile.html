7 stops every first-timer should make in and around Naga

New visitors will find the 'Queen City of Bicol' full of surprises

By Kara Santos and Mia Rodriguez

Naga City draws tourists in with its small-town charm and big-city comforts. Its central location makes it an easy base for exploring islands and mountains in Camarines Sur, but the city itself also has many charms to entice travelers.

Photos: RG Medestomas

1. Naga Cathedral

The Naga Metropolitan Cathedral, founded in 1843, is a customary stop to honor Our Lady of Peñafrancia or Ina, the Patroness of Bicol. Religious antiques are displayed in a museum in the picturesque seminary beside it.

2. Bigg's Diner, Barlin

Homegrown eatery Bigg's has been dishing out comfort-food staples like cheeseburgers, ribs and chicken cordon bleu since the '80s. The well-loved restaurant chain has branches all over Bicol, but the Barlin outpost across from the church serves a taste of history along with the bites. Check out the Spanish-era tunnel beneath the dining area's floor.

3. Naga City People's Mall

Take a 15-minute stroll past town plazas to explore the Naga City People's Mall. Once known as Asia's largest public market, this palengke is the quintessential stop for pili nut delicacies, abaca slippers and baskets at bargain prices. Navigate the maze of thrift shops and stacks of second-hand books on the second floor.

4. CamSur Watersports Complex (CWC)

A popular base for exploring Camarines Sur, CWC offers adrenaline-pumping activities of varying levels. Feeling brave? Fly across the water on a wakeboard or zip across rough trails on an ATV. You can also swim in the pool or challenge your friends to a game of pickleball.

5. Tumaguiti Falls

A well-maintained trail inside Consocep Nature Park leads to the foot of Tumaguiti Falls, where clear water cascades over smooth rocks into a shaded basin.

6. Siruma

Two hours away from Naga, the coastal town of Siruma offers rolling hills, scenic coastal views, and a peaceful countryside atmosphere.

7. Lahus Island

The Caramoan Peninsula is dotted with massive karst formations rising from clear aquamarine waters. Lahus Island is known for two limestone islets connected by a narrow strip of white sand, making it one of the most scenic destinations in Camarines Sur, especially during late afternoon.

Fall in love with Naga and its surrounding landscapes. Book a flight with Cebu Pacific today.

3 Tokyo cafes worth the buzz

From wake-me-up espressos to milky concoctions, here's where you can fuel up

By Trixie Zabal-Mendoza

There’s no shortage of coffee spots for the caffeine hound in Tokyo. The myriad of choices also reflects the allure and energy of the city itself: dynamic, quietly cool and varied, but there’s also a layer that invites you to relax and sit down to a cup of coffee. From no-nonsense cafes to hip Insta grid-worthy spaces, Tokyo certainly has you covered.

Photo: Candice Maddamba Dee

1. About Life Coffee Brewers

The popular Dogenzaka outpost is more like a coffee stand than a café, but the selection of beans and roasts are enough of a draw for coffee fans. They use beans from local roasters (including beans from the equally popular coffee stand, Onibus Coffee, as it is part of the Onibus Coffee group) for its hand drip coffee, espresso menu and milky options. In the more spacious Shibuya branch, you may sit by the bar, inside the shop or on the benches down the side of the café. Both the space and the coffee pose an invitation to sit back and watch the world go by.

1-19-8 Dogenzaka, Shibuya-ku, Tokyo, Japan | Chome-17-1 Shibuya, Tokyo, Japan

Photo: Candice Maddamba Dee

2. Bongen Coffee

Don’t be deceived by Bongen’s small space: its coffee menu lists the usual suspects, but also offers spins on the lattes, mixing it up with hojicha and matcha. Bongen Coffee will remind you of a traditional Japanese restaurant more than a café (spy the lone bonsai plant behind the barista), and it’s the perfect pit stop for a coffee, amidst the bustling shopping energy of Ginza.

2-16-2 Ginza, Chuo-ku, Tokyo

Photo: Trixie Zabal-Mendoza

3. Fuglen Coffee

An Oslo, Norway import, Fuglen surprisingly blends in with the city vibe of Tokyo. Part café, part cocktail house located in a quiet backstreet near Yoyogi Park, Fuglen’s espresso is really the star during daytime (it switches to its bar persona at night and cocktails are served 7pm onwards). The cafe is outfitted with modernist furniture, but one of the best seats in the house is Fuglen’s outdoor benches, perfect for sipping on your cup on a spring day or to soak up the sunshine on a cool December morning.

1-16-11 Tomigaya, Shibuya, Tokyo

San Vicente, Palawan is the beach trip you didn't know you needed

Spend your days lounging by the beach or go island hopping around Port Barton

By The Smile Team - Jen Chan

If you love the beach, you're probably no stranger to Palawan. Consistently voted as a top island destination by industry experts all over the world, it's home to many white-sand beaches as well as a UNESCO World Heritage Site. Despite its popularity, Palawan still has a few places that are relatively under the radar. Just three hours away by car from the Puerto Princesa Subterranean River National Park lies San Vicente, the first Flagship Tourism Enterprise Zone in the Philippines and the only municipality that can boast having the longest beach in the entire country. Read on to find out what you can do here once you visit.

Photo: Andrei Bortnikau / Shutterstock.com

1. Check out the popular Long Beach

Offering an uninterrupted 14.7km stretch of powder-fine sand, Long Beach spans four different barangays, namely: Poblacion, New Agutaya, San Isidro and Alimanguan. While there are some beachfront establishments here and there, most of the shore remains uncluttered. Go swimming, kayaking and paddle boarding or just lay down a beach towel and catch the sunset by the shore – there’s plenty of space to enjoy it all.

Photo: Simon Dannhauer/ Shutterstock.com

2. Go island hopping around Port Barton

Port Barton’s proximity to other smaller islands makes it a popular jump-off point for those who are interested in snorkeling and island hopping. See magnificent corals and fish at Twin Reef or have lunch at one of the nearby beaches. Don’t worry about where to get your food; most tours already come with a delicious seafood feast.

Photo: @turtlebeachpalawan

3. Try the local chao long

Speaking of food, Palawan has another specialty you have to try. Chao long, as its name suggests, traces its origins from Vietnam, specifically, from the Vietnamese refugees who settled on the island after the Vietnam War. It’s a version of the Vietnamese pho that has been adjusted to suit the sweeter Filipino palate thanks to the addition of banana ketchup. Most times, this beef noodle stew even comes with a boiled egg. There’s no shortage of chao long houses in Palawan, but if you’re near Alimanguan, beachfront Turtle Beach Palawan serves a pretty good bowl.

Find reasons to smile when you visit these places in the Philippines

Leave your worries behind

By The Smile Team - Jen Chan

A great vacation starts with the right destination, and the Philippines, recently voted the second happiest country in Southeast Asia, certainly makes a good case for being just that. The people are warm, the food is good and the natural beauty is abundant. The only problem is deciding where to go first, but we have a few ideas.

Photo: John Tiu

1. Divisoria, Metro Manila

If you love shopping for bargain finds, there’s no better place to do so than Divisoria. Located in downtown Manila, it’s got everything you could ever want – from children’s toys to household items. Its four large shopping centers, Tutuban Center, 168 Shopping Mall, 999 Shopping Mall and Dragon 8 Shopping Center, are a maze of never-ending stalls, but you can also hit the street market outdoors for a more authentic experience. Like any market anywhere in the world, Divisoria can get crowded, especially during weekends, so make sure to keep your valuables secure.

Photo: Jilson Tiu

2. Iloilo City, Iloilo

Recently named a UNESCO Creative City of Gastronomy, Iloilo is one of the best places to experience the ingenuity of Filipino cuisine. It’s home to many popular and well-loved Filipino dishes, including batchoy, a filling broth typically served with round noodles, pork innards and beef loin. Everyone has their favorite batchoy place – be it Ted’s, Deco’s, Netong’s or Alicia’s – but one thing’s for sure, you’ll never go hungry here.

Photo: MDV Edwards / Shutterstock.com

3. Panglao, Bohol

If you love the beach, Panglao should be on your bucket list. Known for its white sands and turquoise waters, it's exactly the kind of tropical vacation you've always dreamed of. Panglao also has excellent diving sites. If you enjoy snorkeling or freediving, look up Napaling Point, a place known for beautiful corals and sardine runs.

Dim sum is a gastronomic category all on its own. In Hong Kong, almost all restaurants ensure that this Cantonese culinary treasure dances on the taste buds with its delicate flavors and artistic presentation. Here are our top picks of eateries and shops to go for the premium bite.

3/F, 1 Duddell Street, Central, +852 2525 9191

Under the careful watch of chef Siu Hin-chi, known for his painstaking recipes and meticulous seafood preparation, Ying Jee Club was awarded its first Michelin star after its opening in 2017 and earned its second only a year later. The venue’s focus on Cantonese cuisine paired with an expansive collection of fine wines delights discerning palates with beautifully crafted dishes, including the whole abalone puff with chicken and crispy puff with Wagyu beef.

7 tips for picking an accommodation that fits your needs

At the end of a busy day exploring a new city, lay your head where you're most comfortable

By The Smile Team – Hannah Ooi

How do you choose suitable accommodation now that travel is back? The usuals – like location and reviews – are still up there, but these are the new factors you need to consider when booking a place to stay in a foreign city.

1. Ensure your safety

Gone are the days when Instagram photos were enough to make you book a place to stay. You'll want to choose accommodations that take their sanitation and safety protocols seriously – disinfecting and ventilating before the next guests use a room, enforcing physical distancing in common areas and requiring their staff to wear face masks. They should have sanitizer and alcohol dispensers in contact spots like the reception area and more frequent cleaning of elevators and other high-traffic areas.

Also, ask about the procedures they have in place in case you or any other guest falls ill while staying at the facility. They should have isolation protocols and guidelines for contacting medical professionals and local authorities. Bonus points for contactless check-in and windows that can open to allow air to recirculate.

2. Scope out the area

Choosing your accommodation based on its proximity to well-known landmarks and transport options is always a good idea. A simple search of your city of choice on Google Maps will yield hotel pins, making it easier to plan directions. The only downside to prioritizing location is usually the price point – but in specific destinations where safety could be an issue, especially at night, remember that your well-being is priceless.

3. Get the whole picture

Remember to check when your accommodation of choice last updated its website and its images – if you're looking for a regularly maintained place, the benchmark should be four years. Hotel review website Oyster.com reminds that even so, photographs can lie. Browse its extensive hotel reviews and images written and taken by its "investigators" for a more balanced perspective.

4. Be wary of too-good-to-be-true room rates

About to click "confirm booking" on that sweet hotel deal you saw online? Take a moment to check if the pre-discount rates are accurate. Make sure you compare rates using tracking of historical prices to determine whether a too-good-to-be-true discount means anything, advises Hotel Planner's CEO and co-founder Tim Hentschel.

Hotel search platform Trivago offers a similar price index which helpfully charts the monthly and yearly rates of over two million hotels worldwide. Also, pay attention to the fine print, like their cancellation and refund policies – you never know what will happen!

5. Determine the accommodation type you want

We all know the simple joy of relaxing in a tried-and-tested hotel, but the rise in popularity of vacation rentals over the last decade can be attributed to many happy bonuses: it's a more casual form of holiday stay; there's generally more space for you and your travel mates; there might be facilities you'd be hard pressed to find in a small hotel room (a kitchenette, for one!). Rental marketplaces online include the all-popular Airbnb, Homeaway and Tripping.com, where you'll find options ranging from walk-up apartments to terrace houses and country cottages.

6. Compare amenities

Whether you're looking for a luxury getaway or a wallet-friendly break will determine how necessary some accommodation amenities are – and this is helpful when narrowing down the price range of potential stays. Online travel agency Travelocity allows you to compare the amenities, ratings and room types of up to four hotels side by side.

But is it even safe to use pool facilities these days? The World Health Organization says yes, with certain restrictions. The organization recommends keeping an eye out for the following at swimming pools, gyms, beaches, spas, saunas and steam bath facilities:

  • a maximum number of people allowed to ensure adequate physical distancing
  • fabric mask policies required by local or national regulations
  • hand washing stations, especially in the toilet and change room areas
  • single use towels only
  • a bin for guests to place their towels after use for laundering
  • individual use of drinking water
  • tissues and waste containers with lids
  • high touch areas such as door handle disinfected regularly throughout the day

7. Read and review

That great hotel stays reviewed in October 2016 could be a different experience in 2020. Always research, no matter the accommodation type; even luxury hotels can get a bad rap. Reviewing is also a two-way street: Remember to leave your comments after your stay so that others can have the chance to experience what you had – or, in some cases, be warned well in advance!

Here's an evergreen checklist for safe and happy trips

Infectious disease specialist Dr Issa S Tang goes over the basic hygiene practices every traveler should follow, even when there's no threat of an outbreak

By The Smile Team – Anna Felipe

Good personal hygiene is still the primary way to prevent the spread of infections. Here, Dr Issa S Tang, an infectious disease specialist, lists the basic practices every traveler should keep handy at all times.

1. Wash your hands with soap and water.

Handwashing remains one of the simplest ways to keep germs away. Do it regularly and especially before, during and after handling and eating food; before and after caring for someone who is sick with vomiting or diarrhea; before and after treating a cut or wound; after using the toilet; after changing a child's diaper or cleaning him up in the toilet; after blowing your nose, coughing or sneezing; and after touching garbage.

2. Keep an alcohol-based hand rub on hand.

Alcohol and hand sanitizers are your go-to's when soap and running water are not immediately available. Remember to let the hand rub-dry on your skin after application, and wash your hands as soon as possible.

3. Practice proper cough etiquette.

Cover your mouth and nose with a tissue when you cough or sneeze, and throw your used tissue away as soon as possible. If tissue is unavailable, cough or sneeze into your upper sleeve or the inside of your elbow – not your hands.

4. Keep your distance.

Stay at least three to six feet away from people you meet outside your home. You can do so by picturing yourself and the other person with both arms stretched out – that's how far you should be standing away from one another. Changi Airport also helps remind kids to practice social distancing with these inventive "wings" they could decorate and run around with. Clever and fun!

6 tips to pack a carry-on bag

Learn how to maximize space and organize your belongings better with these useful tips

By The Smile Team – Delle Chan

Packing for your carry-on bag may seem simple enough – just throw in your clothes, shoes and other items higgledy-piggledy, and you're good to go! But how can you maximize the limited space you have in your luggage, and organize your belongings more effectively? Take our tips:

1. Measure your bag

Make sure that your cabin bag falls within the carry-on dimensions specified by the airline. The maximum dimensions for Cebu Pacific are 56cm x 36cm x 23cm for Airbus flights; 56cm x 35cm x 20cm for ATR flights.

2. Check your liquids

Ensure that your liquids do not exceed 100ml, and are packed in a clear ziplock bag. Place them in the outer compartment of your carry-on, for easier removal during security screenings.

3. Roll your clothes

Whether it's a blouse or a long-sleeved polo shirt, or even a skirt, rolling your clothes saves space, and also ensures that they remain wrinkle-free.

4. Compartmentalize

Organize your belongings into soft pouches, to make them easier to find. Cables and cords can go into sunglass cases.

5. Employ space-saving hacks

Stuff underwear, socks and other small items into shoes. Place belts inside the collars of formal shirts – this also helps to keep the collars stiff.

6. Wear your heaviest and bulkiest items

This ensures that you don't have to squeeze them into your bag, freeing up space for other essentials.

5 tips to eat street food safely without getting sick

Eat all the delicious street food that you can and prevent tummy troubles with a few guiding principles

By The Smile Team – Lester Ledesma

An unexpected trip to the emergency room after a bout of food poisoning is never the experience you would want to have when visiting a foreign city. From using properly sanitized cutlery to only chugging down drinks from sealed containers, these safety tips will let you enjoy yummy street cuisine without worry.

1. Eat where the locals eat

Ask a local where his favorite hawker stall is, or you can simply follow the crowd. Natural selection states that food businesses with the most customers usually serve the best eats. This constant turnover of supplies also ensures that the food items on offer are sold long before they get stale. You might have to wait in line, though, and you can easily avoid this by arriving an hour before or after the peak times when the queues are shortest.

2. Steer clear from heavy traffic

Many street food peddlers set up shop at locations along busy footpaths that are typically accessible to commuters. Thus, food contamination from public vehicles' dust and smoke is highly likely. If you really want to try roadside grub, buy it from stalls in well-ventilated areas where food is far from harmful fumes.

3. Watch them wash

Sometimes the danger of the "traveler's tummy" comes not from the street food itself but from the plates and cutlery that the food vendors use. Before ordering that delicious-looking treat, take a quick peep at how the sellers wash their utensils to get an idea of their hygiene standards. Some folks simply dunk their glasses in a bucket of murky water (in which case, stay away!). Others use soap on tap (good) or simply go with disposable containers (highly recommended). Whether you're eating on the street or at a Michelin-starred restaurant, clean plates and utensils are absolute musts.

4. Handle with care

The best, most professional hawkers prepare their delicacies like any other proper restaurant: by using clean hands or tongs, and lots of TLC. Properly boiled, fried, steamed or grilled food is almost certainly risk-free, but the pre-cooked items need more judicious handling. Does the seller touch his merchandise with the same bare hands he uses to collect money (bad)? Does he keep his food covered (good)? And watch out for flies. If too many of them are around, the seller isn't holding his place clean.

5. When in doubt, drink packaged drinks

That glass of water may come free, but don't drink it unless you're sure its content came from a sealed container. The same goes for the ice that usually accompanies a drink – if you're doubtful of its cleanliness, only consume beverages from a can or a bottle.

3 restaurants worth flying for in Manila, Pampanga and Cebu

One of them might just end up with a Michelin star

By Trixie Zabal-Mendoza

The Michelin Guide is finally making its way around the Philippines and foodies, restaurateurs and Filipino food champions across the country couldn’t be more excited. In an announcement last February 18, the French culinary guide stated that they will expand to “Manila and its environs” for its 2026 selection. Anonymous Michelin inspectors will reportedly head to Makati, Manila, Mandaluyong, Parañaque, Pasay City, Quezon City, Taguig, San Juan and even as far as Pampanga, Tagaytay, Cavite and Cebu in search of establishments to add to the Michelin list.

Traveling for food has always had its appeal. In fact, it was in the spirit of adventure that the Michelin Guide was first created. Once a tire company’s marketing tool encouraging people to travel more, the Michelin Guide has evolved to become a guidepost for gourmands with its respected restaurant rating system. While we won’t know which spots will make it to the first list for the Philippines until 2026, in the same spirit of adventure, here’s a list of places worth going the extra mile for:

Photo: Metiz

Metro Manila

1. Metiz

Tasting menus might seem intimidating to most, but Metiz’s eight-course version offers a touch of home by letting familiar flavors shine through food preservation. Think punchy dishes with buro (fermented food), tuyo (dried fish) and kinilaw (cooking food in vinegar and acidic fruit juices to bring out flavors).

Metiz is located at Karrivin Plaza, Ground Floor Building A, 2316 Chino Roces Avenue Extension, Makati City.

Photo: Toyo Eatery

2. Toyo Eatery

Toyo Eatery’s first claim to fame when it initially opened in 2016 was its salad creation dubbed “Bahay Kubo”, which incorporates the 18 vegetables mentioned in the Filipino folk song of the same name. Its tasting menu has evolved and changed since then but the kitchen, helmed by chef Jordy Navarra, is still staunchly nationalistic in its cooking approach and both playful and inventive with what is served on that table.

Toyo Eatery is at The Alley at Karrivin, Karrivin Plaza, Chino Roces Extension, Makati.

Photo: @seaj0725

3. Manam

If pork sisig and watermelon sinigang are not reasons enough to have your next meal at Manam, let its other Pinoy menu items seal the deal. Manam’s numerous branches located in malls around the metro make it easy to visit. Consistency of food quality is also top-notch, making every meal a satisfying and affordable one. Don’t let the long queue discourage you; your stomach will thank you later.

Manam branches are located in various malls in Metro Manila.

3 things to love about the Philippines

Find out why everyone wants to visit this tropical paradise

By The Smile Team - Jen Chan

Deciding where to spend your vacation can be a challenge, especially with so many places on social media already vying for your attention, but if you’re looking for more than a picture-perfect destination – if what you want is a true escape – then there’s really only one choice. Read on to find out five things to love about the Philippines and why you should have booked your flight there yesterday.

Photo: travelwild / Shutterstock.com

1. The beaches are stunning

To say that the Philippines is known for its beaches is like saying that the sun rises and sets every day – it’s just fact. Palawan’s beaches, in particular, are famous worldwide. Head to Coron for powder-fine white sand, secret lagoons and the best wreck diving this side of the ocean. Visit San Vicente for island-hopping in Port Barton and the country’s longest stretch of white sand. There’s also Honda Bay in Puerto Princesa, perfect for island-hopping and snorkeling.

Photo: Crystal Eye Media / Shutterstock.com

2. The culinary scene is 10/10

In the Philippines, each region – sometimes, each city – will have its own special dish. In Cebu, the one must-try is the ubiquitous lechon or suckling pig, its crispy skin and juicy meat making it the star of many an important feast or celebration. However, don’t sleep on the Visayan province’s other delicious offerings either. Try the ngohiong, five-spice-seasoned ground pork or shrimp dipped in crunchy batter and deep-fried to perfection, or if you’re feeling adventurous, tuslob buwa, pig brain sauteed in garlic, onions, chili and shrimp paste.

Photo: Goinyk Production / Shutterstock.com

3. The level of biodiversity is astounding

The Philippines is home to a diverse range of flora and fauna, with some 70% of the world’s plant and animal species taking up residence in the archipelago.
